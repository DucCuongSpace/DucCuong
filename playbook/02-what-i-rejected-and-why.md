# What I rejected and why

- Pure mass-scale AI content generation without quality controls: Jake Ward advocates creating thousands of AI pages (like 5,000 glossary-style or "X vs Y" pages) and tweaking the model until output is "good enough" (source: Jake Ward, https://www.seocasestudy.com/seo-examples/ai-seo-case-studies from 30.01.2026). Rejected because this directly contradicts Lily Ray's evidence-based warning that high-volume AI content backfires when pages are thin, repetitive, or lack clear EEAT, as both Google and AI platforms increasingly penalize "SEO-spam" and reward depth, trust, and usability (source: Lily Ray, https://www.affiliatesummit.com/blogs/the-state-of-ai-and-seo-in-2026-with-lily-ray from 12.02.2026).

- Using AI-generated frameworks and workflows as authoritative systems: Ruben Hassid recommends heavily leaning into AI to design content systems, workflows, and entire websites, treating AI-generated prompts and templates as core building blocks for rapid growth and virality (source: Ruben Hassid, https://ruben.substack.com/p/master from 12.10.2025). Rejected because Rand Fishkin's research demonstrates that AI tools give wildly inconsistent SEO and brand-recommendation lists that can't be trusted at face value; instead, you must design and test your own frameworks (source: Rand Fishkin, https://searchengineland.com/ai-recommendations-inconsistent-fix-469250 from 17.02.2026). AI recommendations are noisy and context-blind, making them useful only as hypotheses, not production-ready systems.
