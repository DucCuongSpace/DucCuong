# Where experts disagree

- Here are three examples where experts in and around AI‑powered SEO content production disagree on the right approach:

1st example: How much to rely on mass‑generated AI content
+) Jake Ward (Byword.ai) recommends:
Use AI to generate thousands of SEO pages quickly, especially for things like glossaries, formulas, and ‘X vs Y’ posts, to reach big traffic numbers (source: [Jake Ward], [https://www.seocasestudy.com/seo-examples/ai-seo-case-studies from 30.01.2026])

+) Ruben Hassid (EasyGen / LinkedIn) recommends:
Using AI to scale consistency and iteration, but keeping the human heavily in the loop; he stresses that “mass‑generating with AI usually means low quality” and that editing, unique angles, and final polish are what make content work. (source: [Ruben Hassid], [https://www.linkedin.com/posts/ruben-hassid_i-get-39-million-views-every-90-days-with-activity-7364325418109231105-EtjB from 21.08.2025])

Which side I take and why? 
- I prefer using AI at scale, but keep humans in charge of taste and quality
- Use Jake Ward’s idea of structured, schema‑based AI content for programmatic pages, but don’t rely only on AI to run your whole content strategy, and follow Ruben Hassid’s approach that humans stay in the loop to add angles and edit AI outputs, so your AI content is scalable but still feels real and builds trust

2nd example: Can we trust AI’s own “SEO advice”?
+) Rand Fishkin recommends:
  AI tools give wildly inconsistent SEO‑ and brand‑recommendation lists, arguing that you can’t trust an AI’s “top‑ranking” or “best practices” output at face value; instead, you must design and test your own frameworks. (source: [Rand Fishkin], [https://searchengineland.com/ai-recommendations-inconsistent-fix-469250 from 17.02.2026])

+) Ruben Hassid (EasyGen / LinkedIn) recommends:
He heavily leans into using AI to design content systems, workflows, and even entire websites, treating AI‑generated prompts and templates as core building blocks for rapid growth and virality (source: [Ruben Hassid], [https://ruben.substack.com/p/master from 12.10.2025])

Which side I take and why? 
- I agree with I Rand Fishkin’s idea: AI is great for exploring ideas, but its recommendations are noisy and context‑blind, so they should be treated as hypotheses, not reality.

3rd example: SEO quantity vs SEO quality
+) Jake Ward (Byword / AI‑driven SEO) recommends:
 Mass‑scale AI‑generated content, e.g., creating thousands of pages (like 5,000 glossary‑style or “X vs Y” pages) to exploit AI‑driven search and long‑tail queries, then tweaking the model until output is “good enough.” (source: [Jake Ward], [https://www.seocasestudy.com/seo-examples/ai-seo-case-studies from 30.01.2026])

+) Lily Ray (Algorythmic / AI Search) recommends:
She emphasizes that traditional SEO and EEAT are still the foundation of visibility in AI‑powered answers; she focuses on trustworthy, authoritative, brand‑aligned content, not on blasting out huge volumes of AI‑only pages (source: [Lily Ray], [https://www.affiliatesummit.com/blogs/the-state-of-ai-and-seo-in-2026-with-lily-ray from 12.02.2026])

Which side I take and why? 
- I lean toward Lily Ray’s view: high‑volume AI content can backfire if pages are thin, repetitive, or lack clear EEAT, because both Google and AI‑platforms increasingly penalize “SEO‑spam” and reward depth, trust, and usability
